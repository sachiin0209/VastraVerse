Women's Traditional Wear

Saree

Pan-Indian

A graceful unstitched drape varying from 5 to 9 yards, wrapped around the waist with one end draped over the shoulder. The garment showcases regional diversity in draping styles, fabric, and patterns.

Regional Variations:

  • Kanjivaram (Tamil Nadu)
  • Banarasi (Uttar Pradesh)
  • Paithani (Maharashtra)
  • Chanderi (Madhya Pradesh)
  • Sambalpuri (Odisha)

Typical Occasions:

Weddings, religious ceremonies, festivals, formal events

Lehenga Choli

North India

A three-piece ensemble consisting of a voluminous ankle-length skirt (lehenga), a fitted blouse (choli), and a dupatta (scarf). Known for intricate embroidery and embellishments.

Types:

  • Rajasthani Lehenga
  • Gujarati Chaniya Choli
  • Bridal Lehenga
  • A-line Lehenga
  • Mermaid Lehenga

Typical Occasions:

Weddings, engagement ceremonies, Garba/Dandiya nights, festive celebrations

Salwar Kameez

North/North-West

A versatile outfit comprising loose trousers (salwar), a long tunic (kameez), and a scarf (dupatta). Available in various styles including Punjabi, Patiala, and Anarkali cuts.

Variations:

  • Patiala Salwar (Punjab)
  • Anarkali Suit
  • Churidar Kameez
  • Palazzo Suit
  • Sharara/Gharara (Muslim influence)

Typical Occasions:

Everyday wear, semi-formal events, college/workplace, festivals

Pavadai Dhavani

South India

Traditional Tamil attire for young girls and women consisting of a long skirt (pavadai), a blouse, and a half-saree (dhavani) draped over the shoulder.

Features:

  • Traditional silk fabric with gold zari work
  • Vibrant colors like maroon, green, and gold
  • Often adorned with traditional motifs
  • Modern versions include Langa Voni and Pattu Pavadai

Typical Occasions:

Coming-of-age ceremonies, temple visits, cultural events, festive celebrations

Mekhela Chador

Northeast India

Traditional Assamese attire consisting of two main pieces: the mekhela (lower garment wrapped around the waist) and the chador (upper garment draped over the body and shoulders).

Features:

  • Usually made of muga silk or pat silk
  • Traditional woven motifs and patterns
  • Geometric designs and natural elements
  • Comes in vibrant colors with golden thread work

Typical Occasions:

Bihu festival, weddings, cultural performances, formal events

Kerala Kasavu

South India

The traditional cream-colored saree with golden border worn by Malayali women in Kerala. Also known as "Mundum Neriyathum" when worn in the traditional two-piece style.

Features:

  • Made of handwoven cotton
  • Distinguished by its golden zari border (kasavu)
  • Simple, elegant design with minimal embellishments
  • Traditional version is off-white with gold border

Typical Occasions:

Onam festival, weddings, temple visits, traditional ceremonies

Pheran

Kashmir

A long, loose gown worn by women in Kashmir, typically made of wool or cotton, perfect for the cold climate.

Features:

  • Long, loose-fitting design
  • Often embroidered with intricate patterns
  • Worn with a headscarf (Kasaba)
  • Available in various colors and materials

Typical Occasions:

Daily wear, winter wear, cultural events

Phulkari

Punjab

Traditional embroidery technique from Punjab, featuring vibrant floral patterns on dupattas and suits.

Features:

  • Intricate floral embroidery
  • Bright, contrasting colors
  • Traditionally made on khaddar fabric
  • Various styles like Bagh, Chope, and Til Patra

Typical Occasions:

Weddings, festivals, cultural celebrations

Chaniya Choli

Gujarat

Traditional three-piece ensemble worn during Navratri, featuring a flared skirt, blouse, and dupatta.

Features:

  • Mirror work and embroidery
  • Colorful bandhani patterns
  • Heavy embellishments
  • Matching accessories

Typical Occasions:

Navratri, Garba, Dandiya nights, weddings

Pochampally Ikat

Telangana

Traditional handwoven saree known for its distinctive geometric patterns and double ikat technique, originating from Pochampally village.

Features:

  • Double ikat weaving technique
  • Geometric patterns and motifs
  • Natural dyes and colors
  • Lightweight and comfortable

Typical Occasions:

Weddings, festivals, formal events, cultural celebrations

Bomkai Saree

Odisha

Traditional handloom saree from Odisha, known for its intricate thread work and tribal motifs, combining both ikat and embroidery techniques.

Features:

  • Combination of ikat and embroidery
  • Traditional tribal motifs
  • Rich border designs
  • Natural silk and cotton base

Typical Occasions:

Weddings, festivals, cultural events, formal gatherings

Muga Silk

Assam

Luxurious golden silk native to Assam, known for its durability and natural golden color. Used in traditional Mekhela Chador and sarees.

Features:

  • Natural golden color
  • High durability
  • Traditional Assamese motifs
  • Handwoven technique

Typical Occasions:

Bihu festival, weddings, cultural events, formal ceremonies

Bandhani

Gujarat/Rajasthan

Traditional tie-dye technique creating intricate patterns on fabric, popular in Gujarat and Rajasthan. Known for its vibrant colors and dot patterns.

Features:

  • Intricate tie-dye patterns
  • Vibrant color combinations
  • Traditional motifs
  • Lightweight fabric

Typical Occasions:

Navratri, weddings, festivals, casual wear

Chanderi

Madhya Pradesh

Luxurious handwoven fabric from Chanderi, known for its sheer texture and gold/silver zari work. Used in sarees and suits.

Features:

  • Sheer, lightweight fabric
  • Gold/silver zari work
  • Traditional motifs
  • Blend of cotton and silk

Typical Occasions:

Weddings, festivals, formal events, cultural celebrations

Kalamkari

Andhra Pradesh

Traditional hand-painted or block-printed textile art from Andhra Pradesh, featuring intricate mythological scenes and natural motifs.

Features:

  • Hand-painted designs
  • Natural dyes
  • Mythological motifs
  • Traditional patterns

Typical Occasions:

Cultural events, festivals, formal gatherings, religious ceremonies

Ilkal Saree

Karnataka

Traditional handloom saree from Ilkal, Karnataka, known for its unique border and pallu design, often made with cotton and silk.

Features:

  • Distinctive border design
  • Cotton-silk blend
  • Traditional motifs
  • Unique pallu pattern

Typical Occasions:

Daily wear, festivals, cultural events, formal gatherings

Kosa Silk

Chhattisgarh

Luxurious silk from Chhattisgarh, known for its fine texture and natural colors. Used in traditional sarees and suits.

Features:

  • Fine texture
  • Natural colors
  • Traditional designs
  • Durable fabric

Typical Occasions:

Weddings, festivals, formal events, cultural celebrations

Phanek

Manipur

Traditional wrap-around skirt from Manipur, often worn with a matching blouse and shawl, featuring unique patterns and designs.

Features:

  • Wrap-around design
  • Traditional motifs
  • Handwoven fabric
  • Matching accessories

Typical Occasions:

Cultural events, festivals, daily wear, traditional ceremonies

Men's Traditional Wear

Kurta Pajama

North India

A versatile outfit consisting of a long tunic (kurta) paired with loose-fitting pants (pajama). Made in various fabrics from cotton to silk depending on the occasion.

Variations:

  • Straight-cut Kurta
  • Angrakha Kurta (overlapping front)
  • Pathani Suit (from Peshawar region)
  • Lucknowi Chikan Kurta

Typical Occasions:

Daily wear, festivals, religious functions, casual gatherings

Sherwani

North India

An elegant, long coat-like garment that extends below the knees, typically paired with churidar or straight pants. Known for its intricate embroidery and royal appearance.

Types:

  • Classic Sherwani (knee-length)
  • Achkan Sherwani (longer style)
  • Jodhpuri Sherwani (Bandhgala)
  • Indo-Western Sherwani

Typical Occasions:

Weddings, engagement ceremonies, formal events, festive celebrations

Dhoti

Pan-Indian

A rectangular piece of unstitched cloth, usually around 5 yards long, wrapped around the waist and legs in various styles depending on the region. Often paired with a kurta or angavastram.

Regional Styles:

  • Bengali Style (front pleated)
  • Tamil/South Indian Style (Veshti)
  • Marathi Style (Dhotar)
  • Gujarati Style (Panche)

Typical Occasions:

Religious ceremonies, weddings, daily wear (in rural areas), classical performances

Lungi

South/East India

A garment worn around the waist extending to the ankles. Unlike the dhoti, it's sewn into a tube-like shape. Available in various colors and patterns, it's known for comfort in hot climates.

Types:

  • Open Lungi (similar to sarong)
  • Kaili/Mundu (plain white with border)
  • Checkered Pattern (casual wear)
  • Fancy Lungis (festive occasions)

Typical Occasions:

Daily wear, home wear, informal gatherings, relaxed settings

Bandhgala / Jodhpuri

Rajasthan

A structured jacket with a mandarin collar that buttons all the way to the neck. Originally from Jodhpur, it blends Western tailoring with Indian elegance, paired with matching trousers.

Features:

  • Closed mandarin collar
  • Fitted silhouette
  • Multiple buttons down the front
  • Rich fabrics like silk, velvet, or brocade

Typical Occasions:

Black-tie events, diplomatic functions, high-profile weddings, formal gatherings

Mundasa (Turban)

Pan-Indian

Traditional headgear made by wrapping a long piece of cloth around the head. Styles vary greatly across regions, communities, and occasions, representing honor and dignity.

Regional Styles:

  • Rajasthani Safa (colorful, often pink)
  • Punjabi Pagri (Sikh turban)
  • Mysore Peta (South Indian style)
  • Marathi Pheta

Significance:

Symbol of respect, honor, and identity; varies by region, community, and occasion

Pathani Suit

North-West

A traditional outfit originating from the Peshawar region, featuring a long tunic with a straight cut and minimal embellishments.

Features:

  • Straight cut design
  • Minimal embroidery
  • Often paired with a waistcoat
  • Popular in Afghanistan and Pakistan

Typical Occasions:

Religious gatherings, cultural events, formal occasions

Angarkha

Rajasthan

A traditional upper garment with overlapping front panels, known for its elegant drape and royal appearance.

Features:

  • Overlapping front panels
  • Rich embroidery work
  • Often made of silk or brocade
  • Traditional royal attire

Typical Occasions:

Royal ceremonies, cultural performances, weddings

Dhoti Kurta

Pan-Indian

Traditional combination of a dhoti (unstitched lower garment) with a kurta, worn across India with regional variations in draping styles.

Regional Styles:

  • Bengali Style (front pleated)
  • South Indian Style (Veshti)
  • Maharashtrian Style (Dhotar)
  • Gujarati Style (Panche)

Typical Occasions:

Religious ceremonies, weddings, cultural events, formal gatherings

Achkan

North India

A long-sleeved outer garment reaching below the knees, often worn with churidar pants. Known for its royal appearance and intricate embroidery.

Features:

  • Long length design
  • Rich embroidery work
  • Buttoned front
  • Often paired with churidar

Typical Occasions:

Weddings, royal ceremonies, formal events, cultural celebrations

Mundu

Kerala

Traditional white cotton garment worn by men in Kerala, similar to a dhoti but with a distinctive border and draping style.

Features:

  • White cotton fabric
  • Golden border (kasavu)
  • Simple draping style
  • Often worn with a shirt

Typical Occasions:

Temple visits, festivals, formal events, daily wear

Pheta

Maharashtra

Traditional Marathi turban, often worn during weddings and festivals. Known for its distinctive style and cultural significance.

Features:

  • Traditional headgear
  • Various tying styles
  • Often with decorative elements
  • Cultural significance

Typical Occasions:

Weddings, festivals, cultural events, formal ceremonies

Gho

Sikkim

Traditional knee-length robe worn by men in Sikkim, tied at the waist with a belt. Part of the cultural identity of the region.

Features:

  • Knee-length robe
  • Wide sleeves
  • Belted waist
  • Traditional patterns

Typical Occasions:

Cultural events, festivals, formal gatherings, daily wear

Bakhu

Arunachal Pradesh

Traditional sleeveless long coat worn by men in Arunachal Pradesh, made from yak wool and decorated with intricate patterns.

Features:

  • Yak wool fabric
  • Sleeveless design
  • Traditional patterns
  • Warm and durable

Typical Occasions:

Cultural events, festivals, cold weather wear, formal gatherings

Traditional Accessories

Bindi

Decorative dot worn on the forehead, traditionally symbolizing the third eye in Hindu tradition. Now also a fashion accessory in various designs.

Bangles (Choodiyan)

Circular bracelet-like ornaments worn by women, made from materials ranging from glass to gold, often worn in sets.

Maang Tikka

Ornament worn on the hairline, typically featuring a pendant that rests on the forehead. Essential in bridal attire across India.

Nath (Nose Ring)

Traditional nose ornament worn by women, ranging from small studs to elaborate rings connected to ear chains.

Payal

Traditional anklet with small bells, worn by women during classical dance performances and special occasions.

Kamarband

Ornamental waist belt, often made of gold or silver, adorned with precious stones and intricate designs.

Jhumka

Traditional bell-shaped earrings, often worn in pairs, featuring intricate designs and sometimes precious stones.

Haath Phool

Traditional hand ornament that connects rings to a bracelet, often worn by brides and during special occasions.

Tikli

Traditional forehead ornament, often worn with a chain connecting to the hair. Popular in North Indian bridal wear.

Bajuband

Traditional armlet worn on the upper arm, often made of gold or silver with intricate designs and gemstones.

Rani Haar

Long necklace with multiple layers, often worn by brides. Features intricate designs and multiple strands of pearls or beads.

Matha Patti

Traditional headpiece that extends from the center of the forehead to the hairline, often worn by brides.

Choker

Short necklace that fits closely around the neck, often adorned with pearls, beads, or precious stones.

Armlet

Traditional ornament worn on the upper arm, often featuring intricate designs and gemstones.

Anklet

Traditional ornament worn around the ankle, often with small bells or decorative elements.

Toe Ring

Traditional silver ring worn on the second toe, symbolizing marital status in many Indian cultures.

Traditional Footwear

Juttis

Handcrafted leather footwear with a closed upper and no left-right distinction. Known for colorful embroidery and embellishments, originating from Punjab and Rajasthan.

Kolhapuri Chappals

Handcrafted open sandals made from tanned leather, originating from Kolhapur, Maharashtra. Known for their durability and unique design with toe loop.

Paduka

Ancient toe-knob sandals, considered sacred in Indian culture. Traditionally made of wood, sometimes adorned with silver or ivory for royalty and ceremonial purposes.

Mojaris

Traditional Rajasthani footwear with intricate embroidery and mirror work. Made from leather and often decorated with colorful threads and beads.

Khadau

Traditional wooden sandals worn by saints and ascetics. Simple in design but highly durable, often made from teak or other hardwoods.

Peshawari Chappal

Traditional footwear from Peshawar, known for its distinctive T-strap design and comfortable fit. Popular among both men and women in North India.

Nagra

Traditional Punjabi footwear with a curled toe design. Often worn with traditional attire during weddings and festivals.

Chappals with Bells

Traditional dance footwear with small bells attached. Popular among classical dancers and performers, especially in Kathak dance.

Velvet Slippers

Luxurious footwear made from velvet with intricate embroidery. Often worn with traditional attire during special occasions and ceremonies.
